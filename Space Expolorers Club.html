The Solar System is a vast and complex structure that consists of the Sun, which is a star at its center, and a variety of celestial bodies that orbit around it. This includes eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, and comets. The four inner planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are known as the terrestrial planets due to their rocky surfaces. In contrast, the four outer planets—Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune—are known as the gas giants (with Jupiter and Saturn) and ice giants (with Uranus and Neptune).

The Solar System formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust. The gravitational pull of the Sun keeps the planets in orbit, while the Kuiper Belt and the Oort Cloud contain many smaller objects that also contribute to the solar system's dynamics. Each planet has unique characteristics; for example, Jupiter is the largest planet and has a Great Red Spot, a massive storm, while Saturn is famous for its stunning ring system. Earth is the only known planet to support life, making it a focal point for exploration and research. The study of our Solar System continues to reveal the mysteries of its formation and evolution, as well as the potential for life beyond our planet.
